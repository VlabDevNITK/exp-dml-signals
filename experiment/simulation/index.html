Exploring HAM RADIO : SIGNALS

Objective:

To acquire and understand the use of Ham Radio Emergency signals.



Discription:

Amateur radio operators employ specific emergency signals and procedures to indicate distress, urgency, safety, test concerns, ensuring effective communication in emergency situations.

Distress Signal:In cases of immediate danger, a distress signal is used to urgently seek help. The Morse Code distress call includes three SOS repetitions, the word DE, and the station's callsign said three times. In voice, it's "MAYDAY" said three times, followed by "THIS IS" and the callsign thrice. The distress message must include callsign, exact location, type of distress, assistance needed, and other relevant details. Distress calls take precedence over other transmissions. Stations hearing it must stop potential interference, listen, and offer assistance. Morse Code distress frequency is 500 kHz; voice frequency is 2182 kHz.

Urgency Signal: This signal indicates a very urgent message about the safety of a ship, aircraft, or person. In Morse Code, it's three repetitions of XXX, and in voice, it's "PAN PAN" followed by the urgency message. It takes priority over other communications, except distress, and stations hearing it must avoid interference.

Safety Signal:This signal is sent to provide weather warnings. In Morse Code, it involves three repetitions of the letters T T T, and in voice, it's "SECURITE" followed by the safety message. It takes precedence over other communications, except distress and urgency signals.

Test Signal:Test signals are transmitted for adjusting a transmitter or receiver, lasting no more than 30 seconds. In Morse Code, they consist of three series of VVV, and in voice, it's the figures 1,2,3,4,5 followed by the station's callsign. The Q code for sending a test signal is QSV.
